YggMesh Off-Grid Mesh Networking

View on GitHub

A self-organizing mesh network for when the grid goes down.

YggMesh is open-source firmware that turns low-cost travel routers into a self-organizing, zero-configuration encrypted network.

Nodes discover each other automatically and start routing the moment they power on. The moment at least one node gets internet access, the whole network does too.

Scenarios

City under pressure

Cellular networks go down in a crowded city — no calls, no news, no way to reach the outside world. But a few people in the crowd have YggMesh routers tucked into their backpacks, running off power banks. A few more are plugged in at nearby cafes, shops, and apartments. The routers find each other automatically and form a shared network. People nearby connect to the Wi-Fi like they normally would. Some buildings still have fiber internet — those nodes bring the whole network online. Everyone in the mesh can communicate, read news, and share what's happening — automatically, without any setup, without any central infrastructure.

Village cut off from the world

In a small town, most people rely on cellular LTE. A handful of homes have fiber. Then one day the cellular service goes down — 90% of residents lose their connection. With YggMesh routers plugged in across homes, neighbors' devices find each other wirelessly, forming a neighborhood-wide network. No configuration needed — just plug in. The 10% of homes with fiber automatically share their connection with the whole village. If two neighbors want a faster link, they can run an ethernet cable between their routers — it just works. The more homes that join, the more resilient and fast the network gets.

Off-grid camping

A group is camping deep in the forest, far from any signal. Someone plugs two small routers into power banks. Within seconds, an invisible local network forms across the camp — no accounts, no passwords, no setup. Everyone connects to Wi-Fi and can share photos, send messages, and see the camp map. Later, a car arrives with a portable satellite dish connected to just one of the routers. Without touching any settings, every phone in the camp buzzes with notifications — one internet connection, shared instantly with everyone.

How it works

Buy a YggMesh router — or flash the firmware onto any supported travel router yourself.

Once powered on, each node scans for nearby peers over a mesh radio channel and forms direct, encrypted connections with any it finds. A Yggdrasil overlay routes traffic across all connected nodes, giving every device a stable address reachable by anyone else on the mesh — regardless of which node they are connected to.

Internet sharing is equally automatic. If a node detects a live uplink — via ethernet, USB tethering, or a mobile hotspot — it shares that route with the entire mesh. All other nodes pick it up immediately.

No configuration files. No pairing steps. No central server.
